*Medically reviewed by Dr. Amara Osei, board-certified pediatrician. This article is for general education and doesn't replace individualized advice from your own prenatal care provider.*

The first trimester brings some of the most dramatic hormonal shifts of pregnancy, and with them, a wide range of symptoms — some universal, some that only some people experience, and a much smaller set worth a call to your provider.

## Common and expected

**Nausea, with or without vomiting.** Often called "morning sickness," though it frequently lasts all day. It typically begins around week six and eases by week twelve to fourteen for most people, driven largely by rising hCG and estrogen.

**Fatigue that feels disproportionate.** Progesterone rises sharply in early pregnancy and has a sedating effect. Feeling exhausted after a normal day is common, not a sign anything is wrong.

**Breast tenderness and sensitivity.** Hormonal changes prepare the body for eventual milk production, and tenderness is one of the earliest, most common signs of pregnancy.

**Mild cramping.** Light cramping, similar to menstrual cramps, can accompany the uterus expanding. It's usually not a cause for concern on its own.

**Mood shifts.** Rapid hormonal change, plus the emotional weight of early pregnancy, can produce mood swings that feel more intense than usual.

## Worth a call to your provider

- Heavy bleeding, especially with clotting or significant cramping
- Severe abdominal pain, particularly one-sided
- Fever above 100.4°F (38°C)
- Vomiting so severe you can't keep fluids down for 24 hours
- Dizziness or fainting
- Any symptom that feels, to you, like something is wrong — trust that instinct enough to make the call

None of these automatically mean something serious is happening, but they're the category of symptom your provider will want to know about promptly rather than at your next scheduled visit.

## A note on comparison

Every pregnancy — even a second or third one for the same person — can present differently. Symptom severity isn't a reliable indicator of how a pregnancy is progressing, so try not to read too much into how your experience compares with a friend's or a forum post. Your prenatal care team, with your actual chart and history, is the right source for reassurance specific to you.
