Returning to work after maternity leave gets treated, in most advice, as a logistics problem: childcare, pumping schedules, wardrobe. Those matter. But the part that catches most mothers off guard is how emotionally loud the transition is — and planning for that is just as worthwhile.

## Start the logistics earlier than feels necessary

**Trial-run childcare before day one.** A half-day drop-off a week or two before you return lets your child (and you) adjust without the added pressure of also being on the clock for work.

**Practice the morning routine, once, ahead of time.** Run through the full sequence — wake, feed, dress, out the door — a few days before your actual return. You'll find the friction points while there's still time to fix them.

**If pumping, build the schedule with your body's actual supply in mind,** not just your calendar. Talk to a lactation consultant if you're unsure how to structure breaks around your specific routine.

## Plan for the emotional part too

Many mothers describe the first drop-off as harder than expected, even when they're genuinely glad to be returning to work. Both things can be true — relief and grief, at the same time, about the same transition. A few things that help:

- **Give yourself the actual first week to just survive it**, rather than expecting to be back at full capacity immediately.
- **Have one person you can text the hard moments to**, without needing to explain or justify the feeling.
- **Resist the urge to overexplain your choices** — to childcare providers, coworkers, or yourself. Whatever arrangement you've landed on is allowed to simply be the one that works for your family.

## For the workplace conversation

If you have any flexibility to negotiate, a phased return — a few shortened days before jumping to a full schedule — tends to ease the adjustment more than people expect. It's worth asking, even if you assume the answer will be no.

There's no version of this transition that's entirely smooth. But a plan that accounts for both the schedule and the feelings underneath it tends to land more gently than one built around logistics alone.
