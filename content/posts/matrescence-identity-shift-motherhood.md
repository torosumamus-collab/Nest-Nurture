There's a term, coined by anthropologist Dana Raphael in the 1970s and popularized more recently by reproductive psychiatrist Dr. Alexandra Sacks, for the identity transformation a person goes through in becoming a mother: **matrescence**. It sits alongside adolescence as a recognized developmental passage — except almost no one grows up hearing about it.

## Why the lack of a word matters

Without a name for something, we tend to interpret it as a personal failing rather than a normal process. New mothers who feel unmoored, weepy, uncertain of who they are anymore, or grieving a version of themselves that no longer quite fits, often assume something has gone wrong. Frequently, nothing has. They're in the middle of matrescence — a hormonal, psychological, social, and even neurological reorganization that unfolds over months, not days.

Research using MRI has shown measurable changes in maternal brain structure during this period, particularly in regions associated with social cognition and empathy. This isn't metaphorical. Becoming a mother restructures the brain in observable ways, on top of everything else being renegotiated at the same time.

## What matrescence can feel like

- Missing your pre-baby identity while simultaneously loving your child fiercely — both at once, not a contradiction.
- Feeling like a beginner at things you used to be confident about, including your own body and your friendships.
- A shifting sense of purpose, sometimes disorienting, sometimes clarifying.
- Waves of grief that have nothing to do with regret and everything to do with change.

## What tends to help

Naming it out loud, to a partner, a friend, or a therapist, tends to loosen its grip. So does lowering the bar for what "adjusting well" is supposed to look like — there is no timeline you're behind on. Connecting with other mothers going through the same season, even briefly, can also make the disorientation feel less like isolation and more like a shared passage.

If what you're feeling tips from disorientation into persistent hopelessness, loss of interest in things you used to enjoy, or thoughts of harming yourself, that's a sign to reach out to a healthcare provider — not a sign that matrescence itself has gone wrong, but a sign your care team should be part of this chapter too.

You are allowed to still be becoming someone. That's what this whole stage is.
