The family dinners I remember most from my own childhood weren't elaborate. They were consistent. The same handful of questions, the same easy expectation that we'd all show up to the table, most nights, in whatever state the day had left us in. That's the bar worth aiming for — repeatable, not impressive.

## Start with the smallest possible version

If "family dinner" currently feels like a burden rather than a ritual, shrink it until it's sustainable. That might mean:

- One shared meal a week to start, not seven.
- Ten minutes at the table, not a leisurely hour.
- A simple rotating question ("best part of today, hardest part of today") instead of forced conversation.

A small ritual you keep is worth more than an ambitious one you abandon by week three.

## Make it resilient to bad nights

The ritual that survives a hard week is the one with the lowest setup cost. A few ways to build that in:

1. **Keep a short list of "no-think" meals** — five dinners you can make on autopilot, ingredients always stocked. Decision fatigue is often the real barrier, not cooking itself.
2. **Separate "together" from "homemade."** Takeout eaten at the table together still counts as the ritual. The togetherness is the point, not the cooking.
3. **Let the table be imperfect.** Mismatched plates, one kid who won't sit still, dinner at 5:15 because that's when it worked — none of it undoes the ritual. Consistency beats polish.

## Why it's worth protecting

Research on family routines consistently links shared mealtimes with stronger vocabulary development, better emotional regulation, and lower rates of risky behavior in adolescence — not because of the food, but because of the reliable, low-pressure connection built into showing up together on a regular basis. Kids tend to remember the rhythm of it more than any single meal.

Start smaller than feels impressive. Repeat it more than feels necessary. That's the whole method.
